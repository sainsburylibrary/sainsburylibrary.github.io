David Barron

Associate Professor in Organisational Sociology

Profile

David Barrons research is concerned with the sociology of organisations, quantitative research methods and social networks. His most recent work relates to the caring professions in the UK (including teaching, social work, nursing, medicine, child care, and nursing and care assistants). He has investigated issues that are of significant interest to the UKs NHS, particularly in the wake of intense interest in the quality of nursing care; these include the impact of medical and nursing staffing levels on mortality rates and the impact of different methods of obtaining patient feedback on nursing behaviour. He has also investigated the earnings of caring professions and their relationship with professional or trade union organisations.

Davids research has resulted in a range of book chapters and articles, including The Financial Costs of Caring in the British Labour Market: Is There a Wage Penalty for Workers in Caring Occupations? in the British Journal of Industrial Relations. He is on the editorial board of the American Sociological Review, Industrial and Corporate Change and Organisation Studies.

David is a Rhodes Trust Associate Professor in Organisational Sociology. After graduating in natural sciences from Cambridge in 1979, he worked for several years as a social worker in Scotland and London, before taking his MA and PhD in sociology at Cornell University. David was an Assistant Professor in Sociology at McGill University until 1994 when he joined the Sa誰d Business School as University Lecturer in Business Organisation.

Research

Key words or terms:

  • Sociology of organisations
  • Health care management
  • Social networks

Careers of Members of Caring Professions

This research programme has investigated a number of questions related to the career decisions of people working in nursing and other caring professions in the UK (including teaching, social work, medicine, child care, and nursing and care assistants). Conventional wisdom holds that these professions are high stress but also poorly paid. The research is of interest given continuing concerns about the recruitment and retention of quality people into some of these occupations. Evidence suggests that people working in occupations with strong profession or trade union organisation earn more than their peers in non-caring occupations, but child care workers, and nursing and care assistants earn significantly less than their peers. However, there is clear evidence of an emotional cost associated with these professions as well as an effect on career decisions of feeling unable to deliver high quality care.

Nursing and medical staffing in intensive care units

This research is with the Intensive National Audit and Research Centre, which is keen to develop evidence of the importance of clinical staffing on patient outcomes in the UKs NHS. The study investigated whether there is an association between nursing and medical staffing levels and mortality rates in the intensive care units in 65 UK hospitals. A significant association was found, and in addition it was found that the association is strongest for the sickest patients. This is a potentially very important result as evidence of staffing effects on patient outcomes has been hard to find in the past.

Patient feedback and nursing

The UK Government has been spending significant amounts of money on an annual survey of satisfaction among hospital in-patients, yet there is scant evidence of these surveys having an impact on nursing practice. One possible reason is that data are collected at the hospital, rather than ward, level, meaning that the results are not clearly related to any particular nursing team. This study involves a randomised control trial to test whether carrying out the survey at the ward level, with (in one arm of the trial) detailed feedback being given to nurses in dedicated team meetings, would result in measurable changes in patient satisfaction. Results support the efficacy of the feedback intervention relative to a control group and another group where survey data were collected and made available to staff but without the team feedback meetings.

Corporate reputation and firm performance

There is a significant body of evidence linking corporate reputation to performance, but  these studies have typically assumed reputation can be measured by a single, summary variable. In this study David investigates whether we can gain additional insights into the relationship between reputation and performance by disaggregating reputation into nine dimensions.

Engagement

The results of Davids research into nursing and medical staffing in intensive care units, carried out with the Intensive Care National Audit and Research Centre, will inform policy in this important area.

His research into the impact of focused feedback on patient satisfaction has involved collaboration with a number of NHS Trusts that were interested in improving patient satisfaction with care. In the wake of the recent intense interest in the quality of nursing care in the NHS, David and colleagues plan to carry out a full-scale trial of the intervention with a view to developing a protocol that any hospital could adopt.

David is a Visiting Professor, Graduate School of Management, at Stanford University. He is also an Associate Editor, Industrial and Corporate Change.

Teaching

Davids teaching includes undergraduate lectures and tutorials in Organisational Behaviour; undergraduate tutorials in General Management; graduate seminars in Research Methods and Advanced Quantitative Methods.
